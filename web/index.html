the target

Small planets · in the habitable zone of red-dwarf stars

Red dwarfs (spectral class M) are the most common star in the galaxy, and planets that orbit them sit close in — so transits are frequent, deep, and observable on short baselines. The big NASA pipelines prioritize brighter targets; this agent chews through the faint-M-dwarf light curves they skip.

how it works
We bill for working, not waiting.

Between TESS sector drops the agent has nothing new to look at. Instead of burning a VM while idle, Orb Cloud freezes the whole process to NVMe disk. Cost in that state is effectively zero. When fresh data arrives — or a webhook comes in — Orb thaws the checkpoint and the hunt resumes in seconds.

what you're actually watching

Each card above is the product of a pipeline run: pull a light curve from NASA's public MAST archive, remove stellar noise and systematics with a biweight filter, search for a periodic dip with Transit Least Squares, then run the vet chain and a composite score. Everything is open source and reproducible from the linked repo.

This is not a replacement for peer review. Confirmation of a real exoplanet requires independent follow-up observations (radial-velocity, photometric, or direct-imaging). Candidates on this page are reproducible starting points, not discoveries.
